Introduction

One of the most beneficial forms of technology that has emerged in the past two decades, both from economic and ecological point of view is the technology that converts organic waste into energy. Basically, by using different types of machinery and processes, these technologies convert everything that is Carbon-Hydrogen based into heavy fuel oil, gas, carbon paste…

The Organic Cavitator is a completely new type of machine in this field. One of the significant differences from the competitive technologies is that alike most other (which use mostly one), the OC combines two processes for de-polymerization (molecular decomposition).

It uses both, mechanical and thermal processes to convert the molecular structure of the organic waste for instance into fuels. This is achieved by controlled cavitation. Or, simply said, the Organic Cavitator uses cavitation to decompose molecules of waste into molecules of fuels.

The cavitation process itself was long seen as unwanted byproduct in the mechanical engineering. Yet, in this technology the mechanical movement and the "unwanted" byproduct caviation are the key behind the efficient processing of the waste to energy.

The Reactor is highly adaptable and it can be easily adjusted both on the input as well as on the output side. This enables for the reactor to use different types of municipal waste, waste plastic, waste oil from restaurants or from the automobile industry and even coal, or simply said almost anything that is organic — Carbon based.

Implementations

The complete co-generative machinery complex envisions on the spot facility (waste depot) that is almost completely self sufficient from an energetic point of view.

The Organic Cavitator is the engine that drives an ecological island. It is the key element in the installation on an existing or a new waste depot which will not only turn the landfills green, but it will also make them a valuable energy source for the future. Most of all, alike many other renewable energy technologies, the OC is creating a new perspective on the green energy as not only as self sustainable business, but as a highly profitable business too.

  • For processing of municipal waste, the Organic Cavitator needs a machine for separation of the metals, sand, glass… all non-organic materials that can’t be processed, and a shredding machine. All the byproducts not used in the reactor for production of fuels — metals, glass and hot water steam are sellable goods.

Market Analysis

Overview

The "waste to energy" sector is one of the most interesting and possibly the most profitable sectors in the modern world. Having in mind that there is no cost for the input materials used in any technology of this kind, and even further, the collecting of the input materials is also a source of finances itself, and with the constant increase of the demand for fuels and energy, simply putted — there is no technology that is not profitable or beneficial to the environment.

Yet, again, the fast development of the technology and the interest for higher profits in this business creates a constellation in which any added value, when compared to the competitive technology, is to be highly appreciated.

The waste to energy sector most commonly used technologies — Pyrolysis and Incineration are methods used with centuries, while the implementation in the waste to energy initially started to spread massively around 50 years ago, as a way primarily not to create energy, but to find a solution for the overcrowded landfills. With incineration the lifespan of the landfills has been prolonged, thus the process also creates a byproduct — energy.

The real boom of the waste to energy started with the increased awareness about the effects of the pollution to the global climate that basically turned the attention of the energy industry to the waste, since any king of processing of the waste can’t be as harmful to the environment as it is the simple storing of garbage in landfills.

In the past period, there are numerous new technologies that are in the field of waste to energy, which can be divided into two main categories thermal and non-thermal processing technologies. Amongst them, the implementation of the incineration and pyrolysis technologies is still dominant, although some combined technologies such is the pyrolysis or incineration gasification technologies are also increasingly becoming more popular.

The key in all waste to energy technologies, as it usually is in the energy sector is the efficiency. Basically, there is no system that will work with losses in this field, having in mind that the garbage is the raw material that this technology uses to produce energy, and it brings a substantial income just for taking the garbage. The average price for storing one ton of garbage on a landfill is around 45 Euro.

The inorganic materials such as metals for instance, once separated from the municipal waste, can be sold for additional income. The process also creates hot water/steam that can also be sold to nearby industries for heating purposes.

However, the difference of the levels of efficiency is what it determines the economic interest of these technologies. Depending on the type of the technology, the efficiency varies from 20 to 40 percent and up to 60 percent in cogeneration models. In the OC the efficiency can vary from 40 to 75 percent, depending strictly on the type of input. In cogeneration, it can go up to 85 percent.

That basically means that more fuel is produced from the same quantities of input waste. More fuel, means more electricity and at the end higher profit.

Competition

COMPETITIVE PROCESSES USED IN COMPETITIVE TECHNOLOGY

Incineration based technologies

Incineration is the oldest and the most commonly used waste to energy implementation, starting from ancient times, people have been using waste, usually animal fecal for heating purposes, to the most advanced combustion of organic materials into energy. Incineration may also be implemented without energy and materials recovery, as a way to solve «overcrowded» landfills, but this is increasingly being banned in OECD countries. The incineration means that waste is burned, and the energy released from the process is converted into electrical or thermal(heating) energy.

Modern incinerators reduce the volume of the original waste by 95–96%, depending upon composition and degree of recovery of materials such as metals from the ash for recycling.

Concerns regarding the operation of incinerators include fine particulate, heavy metals, trace dioxin and acid gas emissions, even though these emissions are relatively low from modern incinerators. Other concerns include toxic fly ash and incinerator bottom ash (IBA) management. Incinerators have electric efficiencies on the order of 14–28 percent.

Average price per implementation for this technology 20 million Euro for 2.000 liters per hour capacity.

Existing implementations

Edmonton Incinerator (officially London EcoPark) is a municipal waste incinerator and waste-to-energy power station which burns London's waste to provide electricity for the National Grid. It is located on the River Lee Navigation and bordered by the North Circular Road, in Edmonton.

The incinerator was built between 1971 and 1974, by the Greater London Council. The building is of metal sheet clad construction with a 100 metres (330 ft) reinforced concrete chimney. The incinerator is currently Britain's largest and it handles unrecycled waste from seven London Boroughs: Barnet, Camden, Enfield, Islington, Hackney, Haringey, and Waltham Forest. The waste is converted into electricity, and the facility has 55 megawatts (MW) of installed capacity for electricity production, which is sufficient power to meet the needs of 24.000 households.

Pyrolysis based technologies

Pyrolysis is a process of molecular decomposition that occurs spontaneously at very high temperatures.

This is one of the mostly used processes in the «waste to oil» industry. Basically, these reactors heat up organic waste in order to decompose at molecular level, thus producing biofuels. There are two basic directions in which these technologies develop: higher temperature for higher efficiency in the decomposing process, and lower temperature achieved by working under higher pressure, when aiming for a higher energy efficiency rate. Therefore, the temperatures at which these technologies work varies quite substantially from 350 degrees Celsius to 1.600 degrees Celsius. In average, a pyrolysis type of technology functions at 600 degrees Celsius.

The advantage of this type of technologies is the easy achieving of the decomposing process.

If the temperature is achieved, alike other technologies, in these reactors the processing is on the way. Furthermore, with the abundance of technology available to reach the desired temperature level, the creation of these reactors is quite easy, although expensive.

The main disadvantage is the low energy efficiency rate, which is due to the big temperatures out of which occur losses by the emissions of thermal energy in the environment.

The Organic Cavitator

What’s different than the competitive technology?
— The efficiency, the cost of production and the cost of operation, the adaptability!

One of the main disadvantages of the competitive technologies is the large quantity of gas that is gained trough the processing. This is mostly because, in order to have fuels, the competitive technologies function at higher temperatures, which are also responsible for the creation of the gas. So because of the thermal energy losses, the efficiency of the machines is lower than of that of the OC. Secondly, the creation of gas is unwanted because of storage — gas needs to be stored under pressure in a specifically designed and constructed container, which increases the cost of the processing.

Therefore, the fact that one can control the energy — fuel that it gets in the process, avoiding the production of gas, is a serious advantage. The cavitation process enables the OC to achieve higher rates of molecular decomposition on much lower temperatures. This enables high adaptability on the input, and on the output as well.

The efficiency rate of the OC is much higher than the one of the competitive technology. It ranges from 45 to incredible 75–80 percent depending on the waste that is processed. This is achieved by operating at much lower temperatures than the competitive technology (270℃). Because it operates at very small temperatures, the creation of gas can be almost completely excluded. Competitive technologies have efficiency rate of up to 35 percent.

The cost of production of the OC is an advantage, because many parts of it that are used are of the shelf, while the rest is produced with the usage of standardized metal processing machinery and standardized electrical equipment. The process of production of the reactors is not labor intensive, nor is the process of operation, which enables low operating costs.
